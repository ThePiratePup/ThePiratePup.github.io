Overall, my research is a critical investigation of emerging computational media. Especially important to this investigation are the creative and ethical inquiries that arise as new tech- nologies and tools develop. Artists’ responses and resistance to the expanding prominence of generative artificial intelligence (genAI) is a particular focus of my research. This work brings attention to creative priorities in traditionally computational spaces, operating at the intersection of human-computer interaction, digital humanities, and digital arts.

My dissertation argues that generative artificial intelligence systems are complex data ecosys- tems inhabited by stakeholders with conflicting interests, where ethical and technical consid- erations are integrally intertwined. This framing contrasts dominant narratives which portray genAI systems as all-purpose productivity-enhancement tools. The resistance displayed by artists is central in this intervention, providing perspectives that illuminate workflow frictions and motivational dissonance between genAI developers and the users of these systems. Using ethnographic interviews, I spoke with artists across disciplines to gather their insights from using and resisting generative technologies in creative practice. These interviews produce data especially attentive to friction between creative priorities and computational implementations. For example, research participants often question the power dynamics at play in the develop- ment of generative tools. The dissertation uses thematic analysis to formulate understanding from interview responses and construct models of AI literacy. The first chapter examines resistance to generative systems in creative domains, highlighting an interactive photo-mosaic constructed from the contributions of a community of artists who shared stories of their generative experiences. The second chapter considers the influence of corporate pressures on the adoption of generative workflows, examining low-stakes hobbyist environments alongside professional spaces. This chapters presents a framework for critical AI literacy constructed from creative priorities, centering creative acts and ethical decisions as a lens to consider generative media. The third chapter discusses use-cases where generative practices mirror historical artistic workflows (such as music sampling), highlighting opportunities for generative technologies to empower artists with tools that align with their definition of effective and ethical workflow integration.

This research provides understanding of generative AI in underexplored creative spaces through the construction of critical AI literacy, empirical data, and theorizing genAI as its own medium. Such work provides 1) models for navigating ethical tensions in creative inquiries, 2) lenses for comparing creative and ethical priorities across disciplines, and 3) guidance for further development of generative tools. Further, this work situates emerging media in historical conditions of power and highlights the voices of artists who have expressed particular vulnerability to generative technologies.

Three publications, in particular, provide foundational work for this dissertation. Published in the proceedings of the 2024 CHI conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems, Is resistance futile?: Early Career Game Developers, Generative AI, and Ethical Skepticism investigates a cohort of game development interns grappling with generative workflows alongside their first professional exposure. Accepted into CHI PLAY 2025, How Do You Want to View This? Generative AI, Creative Ethos, and TTRPG Hobbyists examines the low-stakes environment of tabletop roleplaying game hobbyists, highlighting creative navi- gation of ethical inquiries relating to genAI outside of a professional context. Currently an in-progress manuscript for the ACM Designing Interactive Systems Conference, the third study investigates music creators who use generative AI to investigate generative workflows that strongly resemble non-generative historical practice. Also relevant to the dissertation, and presented in the 2024 AIIDE Workshop on Experimental AI in Games, Axes of Characterizing Generative Systems: A Taxonomy of Approaches to Expressive Range Analysis acknowledges the challenge of describing bias present in generative outputs, and explores the application of analysis methods from the domain of procedural content generation.

My next research project is developing a theory of media that centers experience. Media studies often theorize media as material, e.g. Marshall McLuhan’s ”the medium is the message” or Langdon Winner’s ”political artifacts.” An experience-based theory of media responds to the increasingly interactive nature of emerging media (e.g. generative AI and ”gamification”) and provides a lens to better understand experiential design across media contexts. A key contribution to this project is my work developing the term ”procedural ethics.” In my presentation of Ethics of Non-choice: An Exploration of Procedural Ethics in Twelve Minutes and Griftlands at the Digital Games Research Association’s 2023 Morality Play workshop, I discussed how gameplay embodies and encourages certain types of moral experiences—ethical values are embedded into the designed experience of games. Extending this work, I am currently developing a manuscript for a special issue of the journal Well-Played titled Experiential and Experienced Ethics in Gameplay: A Play-Centric Interpretation of Twelve Minutes and Griftlands. This piece further explores how games can evoke morally significant experiences through elements such as gameplay loops.

Overall, this research agenda develops critical models of understanding media, prioritizing creative and ethical inquiries. Such work provides context-specific knowledge for domains of artistic practice such as game development, tabletop roleplaying games, music production, and digital art production where such investigations are underexplored and community members have expressed particular vulnerability to emerging technologies such as generative AI. Further, this work enables effective comparison across domains, both between art forms and across computational and creative contexts. Ultimately, this agenda formulates methods of communicating about media attentive to interdisciplinary priorities.
